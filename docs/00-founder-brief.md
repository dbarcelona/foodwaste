# Founder Brief

## Working concept

Build an Oakland-first food recovery service that picks up safe surplus food from corporate events, catered office meals, and large catering orders, then redistributes that food through trusted local food access partners.

The organization should be designed around a simple promise:

> Make food recovery easier than throwing good food away.

## Recommended starting structure

The strongest near-term fit is a **California benefit corporation or social enterprise pursuing B Corp certification**, paired with nonprofit and municipal partners.

Why this is the default recommendation:

- You want to sell a service to companies and potentially raise venture or impact investment.
- B Corp certification is designed for for-profit companies, not traditional 501(c)(3) nonprofits.
- Companies may pay for logistics, reporting, and compliance support without needing you to be a charity.
- You can still partner with 501(c)(3) food access organizations that distribute food and can provide charitable credibility.

The strongest alternative is a **501(c)(3) nonprofit**, especially if the primary model becomes grants, donations, volunteers, and charitable food distribution rather than paid corporate services.

## Strategic choice

| Question | Benefit corporation / B Corp path | 501(c)(3) nonprofit path |
|---|---|---|
| Can charge companies for pickup service? | Yes | Yes, but must fit exempt purpose rules |
| Can raise venture equity? | Yes | No traditional equity ownership |
| Can issue charitable donation receipts? | No, not directly | Yes, after exemption recognition |
| Can apply for grants? | Some grants | Many more charitable grants |
| Can become Certified B Corp? | Yes | Generally no |
| Best for | Scalable logistics and impact reporting company | Community food access charity |

## Recommended phased path

1. **Discovery and pilot**
   Operate as a pre-formation project or sole founder project while validating donors, partners, insurance, and food safety requirements. Do not issue donation receipts.

2. **Form a California benefit corporation**
   If paid corporate pilots look viable, form a for-profit mission-locked entity and use the B Impact Assessment as the operating standard from day one.

3. **Use nonprofit partners for charitable distribution**
   Sign written agreements with recipient organizations that can accept and distribute food safely.

4. **Evaluate fiscal sponsorship**
   If grants and charitable donations become critical before forming a nonprofit, seek a fiscal sponsor instead of prematurely creating a 501(c)(3).

5. **Apply for B Corp certification after operating history**
   B Lab certification requires evidence. Build policies, metrics, and documentation during pilots so the application is not a scramble.

## First 90-day objective

Run a small Oakland pilot:

- 1 to 3 company or event donors
- 1 to 2 caterers
- 2 to 4 recipient partners
- 5 to 10 pickups
- temperature logs, food type logs, pickup timing, pounds recovered, and recipient feedback
- one public impact report

## Non-negotiables before pickup operations

- Written donor agreement
- Written recipient agreement
- Food safety acceptance policy
- Insurance review
- Driver/pickup SOP
- Temperature log process
- Incident response plan
- Clear rule that unsafe or ineligible food is refused
