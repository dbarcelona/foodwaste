# Business Plan

## Executive summary

Food Recovery Oakland is an Oakland-first food recovery startup concept that recovers safe surplus food from corporate events, catered office meals, and large catering orders, then redistributes it to trusted local food access partners.

The company is not a restaurant discount marketplace. It focuses on high-volume catered surplus where food has already been purchased, is still edible, and is often discarded because the logistics, safety, and reporting workflow is missing.

## Mission

Recover surplus catered food before it becomes waste and move it safely to neighbors who need it.

## Vision

A Bay Area where catered abundance is automatically routed into community care instead of landfill-bound waste.

## Problem

Corporate offices, events, and catered meetings create predictable surplus. The friction starts after the event:

- no clear owner for leftovers
- uncertainty over what can be donated
- food safety risk concerns
- lack of pickup infrastructure
- no recipient partner lined up
- weak reporting for companies that want impact metrics
- fear of liability or reputational harm

Companies may compost food or discard it because that is operationally simpler than donation.

## Solution

A service that handles the operational middle:

- pre-event surplus planning
- donor/caterer food safety guidance
- temperature and chain-of-custody logs
- scheduled and same-day pickups
- recipient partner matching
- delivery confirmation
- impact reporting
- optional equipment and signage kits

## Value proposition

### For companies

Your catered surplus becomes documented community impact.

Benefits:

- employee morale
- local community benefit
- less visible waste after events
- ESG and sustainability reporting
- credible food access story
- potential SB 1383 support where applicable
- reduced staff friction

### For caterers

Food recovery becomes an add-on that improves client experience and reduces waste.

Benefits:

- clear donation workflow
- less food discarded during breakdown
- sustainability differentiation
- partner that handles pickup and documentation
- fewer ad hoc requests from clients

### For recipient organizations

More prepared food arrives with clearer expectations.

Benefits:

- predictable donation windows
- better communication about food type and quantity
- delivery support
- ability to accept surplus without building donor pickup infrastructure

## Revenue model hypotheses

### 1. Per-pickup fee

Companies pay a flat fee for each successful pickup.

Best for:

- one-time events
- pilots
- companies with unpredictable catering schedules

Open question:

- pricing must cover driver time, insurance, packaging, dispatch, failed pickups, and reporting.

### 2. Monthly office recovery plan

Companies pay a monthly fee for a defined number of pickups plus reporting.

Best for:

- offices with weekly catered meals
- workplace teams
- recurring client events

### 3. Event recovery package

Sold as a sustainability add-on for conferences, trainings, or large events.

Includes:

- pre-event planning
- signage
- caterer briefing
- pickup coordination
- recovery report

### 4. Equipment and readiness kit

Companies buy or rent a kit:

- calibrated thermometers
- hot holding guidance
- cold holding guidance
- labels
- signage
- packaging
- donation staging instructions

### 5. Grants

Potentially relevant for:

- pilot operations
- route infrastructure
- refrigeration
- software
- volunteer coordination
- food waste prevention

Source: CalRecycle says its Edible Food Recovery Grant Program keeps food out of landfills and funds projects that collect edible food to feed people or prevent food waste.

## Competitive positioning

### Differentiation

- Oakland-first, corporate catering focus
- operations-led pickup service, not only software
- partner directory and resource hub
- equipment and readiness layer
- local relationship-building with food access organizations
- designed for high-friction prepared food moments

### Competitor categories

- food recovery logistics platforms
- volunteer food runner networks
- food banks and pantry networks
- corporate sustainability vendors
- compost/waste haulers
- catering companies offering internal donation workflows

## Why companies will say yes

Optimistic case:

- they want a tangible local impact story
- employees dislike visible waste
- food recovery helps sustainability goals
- the service makes donation easy
- impact reports are useful for internal communications
- event teams want a repeatable process

Pessimistic case:

- they fear liability
- they do not want extra vendor complexity
- event teams are overloaded
- they already compost
- reporting is not worth paying for
- they worry food donation exposes over-ordering
- procurement cycles are slow

The product must win by reducing risk and friction.

## Operating model

1. Donor submits pickup request.
2. System screens food type, quantity, timing, and holding conditions.
3. Dispatch confirms recipient capacity.
4. Donor/caterer stages eligible food.
5. Driver checks packaging, labels, and temperature where applicable.
6. Driver logs pickup, food type, quantity, and photos if allowed.
7. Food is delivered to recipient partner.
8. Recipient confirms receipt and usable quantity.
9. Donor receives impact summary.

## Key risks

- unsafe food accepted by mistake
- recipient partners lack capacity at pickup time
- companies are unwilling to pay
- logistics costs exceed revenue
- legal/tax positioning is misunderstood
- brand damage from one bad incident
- pickup timing conflicts with event teardown
- overreliance on volunteers for reliability

## Risk controls

- written agreements
- insurance review
- food acceptance policy
- temperature logging
- recipient capacity confirmation
- driver training
- refusal process
- clear donor disclaimers
- no donation receipts unless legally authorized
