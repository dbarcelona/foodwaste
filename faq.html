FAQ

Make the risky questions explicit.

Food recovery only works when donors, caterers, recipients, and drivers know what will and will not happen.

Can this issue tax-deductible donation receipts?

Not unless a qualified 501(c)(3) nonprofit or fiscal sponsor is receiving the donation and issuing the receipt. A benefit corporation should not imply tax deductibility. Donors should consult their own tax advisors.

Is this already a Certified B Corp?

No. The recommended path is to build as a benefit corporation or social enterprise aligned with B Corp standards, then apply once there is enough operating evidence.

What food can be accepted?

The first pilot should focus on food from commercial kitchens with clear handling history, safe packaging, recipient approval, and direct delivery. Unsafe, homemade, contaminated, or poorly documented food should be refused.

Does liability protection eliminate risk?

No. Federal and California donation protections can help, but they do not replace food safety rules, insurance, written agreements, training, or legal review.

Why would companies pay?

Because the service removes friction: pickup logistics, safety documentation, recipient matching, and impact reporting. The product must be easier than disposal and more credible than a vague sustainability claim.

Why start in Oakland?

Oakland is home, has visible food access needs, and is small enough for relationship-led pilot operations before expanding into denser San Francisco office markets.

Open questions should be treated as design inputs, not obstacles.
