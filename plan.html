Food Recovery Oakland
Story Companies Caterers Resources Plan

Working business plan

A B Corp-oriented food recovery service for catered surplus.

Recover edible food from corporate events, deliver it to local partners, and give donors the reporting they need to keep participating.

Problem

Companies order catered meals for meetings and events. When food remains, it often gets discarded because no one has a safe, fast, documented recovery workflow.

Solution

A dedicated pickup and reporting service that connects corporate surplus to local food distribution partners, beginning in Oakland and expanding to San Francisco.

Business model hypotheses

Company-paid pickup

Companies pay per event or subscribe monthly for recurring office recovery.

Grant-funded pilot

Local climate, hunger, or waste-diversion grants fund route development and partner onboarding.

Venture-backed logistics

Funding supports technology, dispatch, data, and expansion into dense office markets.

Equipment and training

Companies buy or rent recovery kits: warming trays, cooling trays, thermometers, labels, and signage.

Why now

  • Food costs and food insecurity remain high across the Bay Area.
  • Companies need credible sustainability actions beyond internal messaging.
  • California SB 1383 creates recordkeeping and edible food recovery expectations for covered generators.
  • Existing organizations can distribute food, but many donors still need pickup infrastructure.

First pilot

  • Recruit one Oakland office or event venue.
  • Partner with one caterer and one recipient organization.
  • Run five pickups with temperature and weight records.
  • Interview all partners after each pickup.
  • Publish a short impact report and refine the pitch deck.

Legal and operating questions to validate

  • Whether the company should start as a corporation pursuing B Corp certification, a public benefit corporation, or another social enterprise structure.
  • How donation receipts can be handled if the entity is not a 501(c)(3), including whether a fiscal sponsor is needed.
  • Which food types are acceptable after being served, held, packaged, or displayed.
  • What insurance, food handler training, vehicle requirements, and local health guidance apply to pickup operations.
  • How to document chain of custody without creating unnecessary friction for donors or recipients.
