Founder journey

From one grain of rice to a recovery route.

Food recovery became personal long before it became a startup idea.

Home

Waste was never invisible.

Growing up, my mom taught me not to leave a single grain of rice on the table. It was not framed as a grand lesson. It was simply how food was treated: with care, gratitude, and memory.

NYC

Food insecurity became visible.

In high school in New York City, I saw how close abundance and scarcity could sit beside each other. Full cafeterias, packed streets, and people still unsure where their next meal would come from.

Learning

The system started to come into focus.

A John Oliver segment on food waste, headlines about food recovery leaders, and stories from programs across the country made the problem feel both massive and solvable.

Experimenting

Even failed projects pointed somewhere.

A science research project using banana peels and onion skins to clean water did not work as hoped. But it was an early attempt to turn what gets discarded into something useful.

Service

Volunteering made the need concrete.

Food pantry work showed the dignity, complexity, and consistency required to feed people well. Food access is not solved by a single donation. It depends on reliable systems.

Berkeley and Oakland

The question followed me west.

Moving to Berkeley and living in Oakland made the contradiction harder to ignore: brilliant institutions, catered events, and thriving companies existing alongside persistent hunger.

Now

The work is to build the missing handoff.

After graduation, corporate events made the opportunity obvious. Good food was being thrown away because no one owned the recovery process. This project is a commitment to own that process.

Food Recovery Oakland is a working concept for a B Corp-oriented food recovery startup.
